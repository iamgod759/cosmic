00 · The cosmic stage

We are made of star stuff

Every element heavier than helium was forged in the cores of stars and scattered across the universe when they died. Your calcium, your iron, your carbon — all born in stellar furnaces. The human story begins not in Africa, but in the hearts of ancient suns.

Roughly 13.8 billion years ago everything — matter, energy, space, and time itself — erupted from a single point. As the cosmos cooled, gravity gathered hydrogen into stars. Those stars cooked light elements into heavier ones, then exploded, seeding space with the raw material of planets and people. Every carbon atom in your DNA was once inside a star.

💥

The Big Bang

All matter, energy, space and time began in a single point 13.8 billion years ago. The universe has been expanding and cooling ever since — from a searing fireball to the star-strewn cosmos we see tonight.

⭐

Stellar nucleosynthesis

Stars fuse hydrogen into helium, then helium into carbon, oxygen and iron — the elements of life. When massive stars die as supernovae, they scatter these elements across space to seed new worlds.

🌍

A planet of life

On Earth, those elements assembled into molecules, then cells, then minds. The same carbon forged in a dying star now forms your DNA. We are, quite literally, the universe made conscious.

Australopithecus

"Lucy" · ~3.2 million years ago

A small-brained upright walker. The brain is barely larger than a chimp's, the face juts forward, and a bony brow shields the eyes. Yet the pelvis and knees are already built for walking on two legs — walking came first, long before the big brain.

Our vanished cousins

We were never the only humans

As sapiens spread, we met other members of our genus — and interbred with them. For a brief window Earth held several human species at once. Then, only we remained. But they live on inside us: most people outside Africa carry ~2% Neanderthal DNA, and some carry Denisovan genes too.

05 · What comes next

The future of the human

For the first time, a species can direct its own evolution. Natural selection hasn't stopped — but culture, medicine, and technology now steer it. Here's where we might be going.

NOW → 2040s

Directed evolution

CRISPR gene editing already cures sickle-cell disease (approved 2023) and lets us correct inherited disorders. As tools sharpen, humanity gains the power — and the ethical burden — to shape its own genome. The line between healing and enhancement is where the next century's hardest debates live.

2030s →

Merging with our tools

Brain–computer interfaces (Neuralink, Synchron) already let paralysed people type and control devices with thought. Our species has always extended itself through tools — from hand-axes to smartphones. The next step brings the tool inside, blurring where the human ends and the technology begins.

2030s → 2100s

Life beyond Earth

If we settle the Moon and Mars, isolated populations in low gravity and high radiation could, over many generations, begin to diverge — the first branching of the human lineage since our cousins died out. Space may do to us what oceans once did to island species.

ONGOING

Still evolving, quietly

We're changing right now: lactase persistence spread in the last 10,000 years, high-altitude adaptations in Tibet arose in ~3,000, and genes for malaria resistance shift generation by generation. Culture is the fastest evolutionary force of all — and it now moves in years, not millennia.

2030s → 2060s

Extending the lifespan

Research into cellular senescence, epigenetic reprogramming, and age-reversal (Yamanaka factors) aims not just to add years but to compress illness into life's final moments. A species that doubled its life expectancy in a single century may not be done yet.

THE DEEPER SHIFT

A global superorganism?

The biggest change may not be biological. Eight billion minds linked in real time, sharing knowledge and coordinating at planetary scale, behave less like separate individuals and more like a single thinking network — a new kind of evolution, running on culture and silicon rather than genes.
