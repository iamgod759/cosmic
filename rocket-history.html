■ SEC 01 · First principles

Why rockets work at all

A rocket is the only machine that carries everything it needs — including its own oxygen — which is why it works in the vacuum of space where jets and propellers cannot.

Newton's Third Law

Action = −Reaction

A rocket doesn't "push against air." It hurls hot gas downward at 2–4.5 km/s, and momentum conservation shoves the vehicle up with equal force. That's it — the entire trick, stated by Newton in 1687.

The Rocket Equation

Δv = Isp · g₀ · ln(m₀ / m𝒇)

Written by Konstantin Tsiolkovsky in 1903. It says speed gained depends on exhaust efficiency (Isp) and how much of your rocket is fuel. Its logarithm is brutal: to go twice as fast you need exponentially more propellant — the "tyranny of the rocket equation."

Staging

Drop mass → gain Δv

The cheat code against that tyranny: once a fuel tank is empty, it's dead weight. Throw it away mid-flight and the equation resets with a lighter rocket. That's why orbital rockets fly in 2–3 stages — an idea Tsiolkovsky called "rocket trains."

■ SEC 02 · Propulsion deep-dive

Inside a liquid rocket engine

This is a cutaway of a pump-fed liquid engine — the architecture behind the F-1, Merlin, and Raptor. Click the numbered points (or the buttons) to trace propellant from tank to fire.

Gas Generator

F-1 · MERLIN · RS-68

A small "engine within the engine" burns a bit of propellant just to spin the pumps, dumping its exhaust overboard. Simple and rugged — the cycle that took Apollo to the Moon.

Staged Combustion

RD-180 · SSME/RS-25

The pump exhaust is fed back into the main chamber instead of wasted, squeezing out several extra percent of efficiency at the cost of ferocious pressures and plumbing.

Full-Flow Staged

SPACEX RAPTOR

ALL propellant passes through two preburners before combustion — cooler turbines, maximum performance. First flown on Starship; only three engines in history have achieved it.

Electric Pump / Pressure-Fed

RUTHERFORD · DRACO

Rocket Lab's Rutherford spins its pumps with battery-powered motors; simpler thrusters skip pumps entirely and push propellant with tank pressure. Small, clean, restartable.

The other kind: solid rocket motors

The oldest rocket design — the same chemistry as a 9th-century Chinese fire arrow, scaled up 100,000×. Fuel and oxidizer are pre-mixed into a rubbery propellant grain cast directly inside the casing. Light the hollow core and the entire inner surface burns at once.

Why the star-shaped channel? Thrust is proportional to burning surface area. A star exposes far more surface than a plain cylinder at ignition — and as it burns outward, the shape (an 11-point star in the Shuttle's forward segment) is engineered so thrust follows a pre-programmed curve, easing off automatically around Max-Q with no moving parts at all.

The trade-off: a solid delivers colossal thrust from simple hardware — but once lit it cannot be throttled, stopped, or restarted. It burns until the propellant is gone. That's why solids serve as strap-on boosters (Shuttle, SLS, Ariane, GSLV) and missiles rather than precision upper stages.

▣ The real thing: NASA/Northrop Grumman's SLS five-segment booster in a ground test at Promontory, Utah — 16 MN of thrust, ~5.5 tonnes of propellant consumed per second, flame at ~3,100 °C.

Shuttle-heritage propellant recipe: 69.6% ammonium perchlorate (oxidizer), 16% aluminum powder (fuel), iron oxide catalyst, all bound in PBAN rubber. It looks and feels like a pencil eraser — and each Shuttle booster carried ~500 tonnes of it.

■ SEC 05 · Trajectory ahead

What the future holds

Rocketry is in its biggest transformation since Apollo. Here's what's on the launch manifest for humanity.

🔁
NOW → 2030

Full & rapid reusability

Falcon 9 boosters already refly 20+ times. Starship aims for both stages returning — with the booster caught mid-air by tower arms, first achieved October 13, 2024. If launch becomes as routine as an airline flight, cost per kilogram could fall 10–100×, unlocking everything below.

🌙
2026 → 2030s

Return to the Moon — to stay

NASA's Artemis program (with SpaceX, Blue Origin, ESA, JAXA, ISRO partners) plans crewed landings and a permanent south-pole base, mining lunar ice for water, air, and — split into hydrogen and oxygen — rocket fuel made off Earth.

🔴
2030s

Humans on Mars

Starship was designed from day one as a Mars ship: refueled in orbit, landing 100+ tonnes, and making return propellant (methane + oxygen) from Martian CO₂ and ice via the Sabatier reaction. The first uncrewed cargo flights are the opening move of a multi-decade settlement effort.

⚛️
LATE 2020s →

Nuclear propulsion

Nuclear-thermal rockets (NASA/DARPA's DRACO program) heat hydrogen with a reactor instead of burning fuel — roughly twice the efficiency of the best chemical engines, cutting Mars transit times and radiation exposure for crews.

🏭
2030s → 2040s

Space industry & mining

Cheap launch makes orbital factories (perfect crystals, fiber optics, pharmaceuticals grown in microgravity), commercial space stations replacing the ISS after ~2030, and eventually asteroid mining — a single metallic asteroid holds more platinum than has ever been mined on Earth.

✨
2040s → BEYOND

Toward the stars

Chemical rockets can't reach other stars in a lifetime — but light can. Concepts like Breakthrough Starshot would push gram-scale probes with giant lasers to 20% of light speed, reaching Alpha Centauri in ~20 years. The rocket equation's final loophole: leave the propellant at home.
