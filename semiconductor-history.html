01 · THE PHYSICS

Conductor, insulator… or negotiable

Whether a material conducts comes down to its band gap — the energy jump an electron must make from the bound "valence band" to the free-roaming "conduction band". Watch the electron in each material:

Conductor

COPPER · GOLD · ALUMINUM

Bands overlap — electrons are always free. Great for wires, useless for logic: you can't switch it off.

Semiconductor

SILICON · Eg ≈ 1.1 eV
← small gap →

A small, jumpable gap. Heat, light, or a voltage can boost electrons across — conduction on demand. This is the magic.

Insulator

GLASS · RUBBER · SiO₂ (9 eV)
huge gap

The gap is a canyon — electrons try and fall back. Perfect for keeping currents apart.

INTERACTIVE · DOPING

Pure silicon: a perfect, boring crystal

Each silicon atom shares its 4 outer electrons with 4 neighbors — every electron is locked in a bond. Pure silicon barely conducts at all. The trick that unlocked the modern world: contaminate it on purpose.

Doping = adding ~1 impurity atom per million silicon atoms
02 · THE DEVICE

The transistor: a switch with no moving parts

Put N and P silicon together and you get diodes, solar cells — and the MOSFET, the transistor design behind ~99.9% of all chips. It's a faucet for electrons: voltage on the gate opens a channel between source and drain. Try it:

Gate at 0 V — the switch is OFF

With no gate voltage, the P-type body blocks electrons: two back-to-back junctions, no path. The output reads a binary 0.

0 µADRAIN CURRENT
0LOGIC STATE
<1 nsSWITCHING TIME
The gate never touches the channel — it works by pure electric field through ~1 nm of glass. Pair an N-type MOSFET with its P-type mirror and you get CMOS, which draws power only while switching — the reason a chip with 20 billion of these doesn't melt. Wire switches into AND/OR/NOT gates, gates into adders, adders into processors: every computation ever run is just this switch, repeated.

07 · WHAT'S NEXT

The future: beyond the nanometer

Classic Moore's Law scaling is slowing — but the industry's roadmap into the "angstrom era" is more inventive than ever.

2025 → 2032

GAA → CFET: the angstrom era

FinFETs are giving way to gate-all-around nanosheets (TSMC N2, Intel 18A, Samsung 3GAE), where the gate wraps the channel completely. Next: CFETs stack the n and p transistor on top of each other, halving footprint again. Roadmaps now speak in angstroms — "A14" means 1.4 nm-class.

2028 →

2D materials & new channels

When silicon channels get too thin, electrons scatter. Materials one atom thick — molybdenum disulfide, tungsten diselenide, carbon nanotubes — could carry current in sheets 10× thinner. Labs at TSMC, Intel, and imec have working 2D transistors; making a trillion of them uniformly is the decade's challenge.

NOW → 2030s

Chiplets, 3D stacking & backside power

If you can't shrink further, build upward. Chiplets (AMD's Ryzen, Nvidia Blackwell) bond multiple specialized dies into one package; HBM memory stacks 12+ layers; backside power delivery moves wiring under the transistors. "More than Moore" — scaling the system, not just the transistor.

2026 → 2035

Silicon photonics

Moving data now costs more energy than computing it. Photonic chips route information as light — co-packaged optics are already replacing copper between AI accelerators, and on-chip optical interconnects could cut datacenter interconnect power by 10×.

2030s

Quantum & neuromorphic silicon

The same fabs now print qubits: silicon spin qubits and cryo-CMOS controllers ride on standard 300mm wafers. Meanwhile neuromorphic chips (Intel Loihi, IBM NorthPole) compute like neurons — memory and logic fused — promising 100× efficiency for AI at the edge.

NOW → ∞

AI designing its own hardware

Google's AlphaChip lays out chip floorplans better than human engineers; Nvidia, Synopsys, and Cadence embed AI across design and verification. The loop has closed: chips train AI, and AI now designs chips — each generation bootstrapping the next. Wide-bandgap materials (SiC, GaN) meanwhile quietly electrify cars and grids.
